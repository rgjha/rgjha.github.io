Nature is Physics, rest is stamp collecting - Ernest Rutherford

I am currently a postdoctoral researcher at Perimeter Institute for Theoretical Physics in Waterloo, Canada. I completed my Ph.D. from Syracuse University in 2019. More details can be found in my CV. My research is related to the study of supersymmetric gauge theories as non-perturbative formulations of superstring/M-theory through the gauge/gravity duality. I am interested in numerical studies of the finite-temperature maximally supersymmetric gauge theories using Monte Carlo (MC) methods to predict results about non-extremal black $p$-branes in the dual supergravity (SUGRA). This constitutes a non-trivial check of the well-known AdS/CFT conjecture which relates $\mathcal{N}=4$ in four dimensions and Type IIB string theory on $AdS_{5} \times S_{5}$. However, even when the field theory is non-conformal (in space-time dimensions $\neq 4$), there is still well-defined region in the dimensionless coupling space where the conjecture holds. I am also exploring phase structures of multi-matrix models with holographic behaviour such as BMN matrix model. I am presently also studying the strongly coupled sector of $\mathcal{N} = (2,2)$ SYM in two dimensions at large $N$. Few years ago, I also started working on tensor networks methods to study lower-dimensional gauge theories such as U(1), SU(2), and spin models exhibiting continuous or discrete symmetries such as two-dimensional (classical) Ising, O(2), and O(3) models. Since the tensor network methods are computationally difficult and not scalable in higher dimensions, I am exploring different algorithms which will enable us to explore more interesting three-dimensional classical statistical models with sufficient accuracy. A long term goal is to compute critical exponents in the these models by exploring the QFT limit and compare to the results from the well-established conformal bootstrap program and MC methods. Another directions is to study models which are affected by sign problems in conventional Monte Carlo methods such as those at finite chemical potential or with topological $\theta$-term. One of the directions which I want to pursue in coming years is to understand the real-time (Minkowski) dynamics of matrix models.
