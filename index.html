Nature is Physics, rest is stamp collecting - Ernest Rutherford

I am currently a postdoctoral researcher at Perimeter Institute for Theoretical Physics in Waterloo, Canada and moving to Thomas Jefferson Lab (JLab) in September 2022 for my second postdoc. I completed my Ph.D. from Syracuse University in 2019.
(Last updated: July 3, 2022, see CV here)
My research interests are related to supersymmetric gauge theories as non-perturbative formulations of superstring/M-theory through the gauge/gravity duality. I am interested in the study of the finite-temperature maximally supersymmetric gauge theories using Monte Carlo (MC) methods to check and predict results about non-extremal black $p$-branes in the dual supergravity (SUGRA). This approach will constitute a non-trivial check of the well-known AdS/CFT conjecture which relates $\mathcal{N}=4$ in four dimensions and Type IIB string theory on $AdS_{5} \times S_{5}$. However, even when the field theory is non-conformal (in space-time dimensions $\neq 4$), there is a well-defined region in the dimensionless coupling space where gauge/gravity duality holds. I am also exploring phase structures of multi-matrix models with holographic behavior such as BMN matrix model and the role of gauge symmetry in these one-dimensional models. I am also exploring the aspects of matrix bootstrap methods (away from zero temperature and planar limit) and how these methods can be combined with MC methods to understand the master field orbits. I wrote a review on numerical and analytical aspects of Hermitian matrix models discussing these topics in late 2021. Another research direction that I have explored are the tensor network renormalization group methods to study lower-dimensional gauge theories such as U(1), SU(2), and spin models exhibiting continuous or discrete symmetries such as (classical) Ising, O(2), and O(3) models. Since the tensor network methods are computationally expensive in higher dimensions, one of my other interests is also to explore different algorithms which will enable us to study a wide class of interesting three-dimensional classical statistical models with sufficient accuracy. A long-term goal is to compute critical exponents in these models by exploring the QFT limit and comparing it to the results from the well-established conformal bootstrap program and MC methods. Another direction is to study models which are affected by sign problems in conventional Monte Carlo methods, such as those at finite chemical potential or with topological $\theta$-term. One of the projects which I want to pursue in the coming years is to understand the real-time (Minkowski) dynamics of matrix models. I am also interested in applications of quantum computation (or more formally -- classical/quantum (hybrid) NISQ based algorithms) to quantum many-body problems.
